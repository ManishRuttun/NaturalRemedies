What are Allergies?

When we think of allergies, we think of constant sneezing, rash breakouts, and shortness of breath. In an otherwise normal setting, these indications represent atypical responses by an individual. So, what are allergies, exactly? Allergies are hypersensitivity reactions triggered by the immune system. Allergic reactions cause an increase in IgE or IgG/IgA/IgM antibodies. Allergies can be triggered by antibody or cell-mediated reactions. IgE-mediated allergic reactions and non-IgE-mediated allergic reactions are two types of antibody-mediated allergies. Allergic rhinitis (hay fever), allergic asthma, and allergic conjunctivitis are only a few examples of IgE-mediated allergic responses. Non-IgE-mediated allergic reactions include, among other things, non-IgE-mediated conjunctivitis, anaphylaxis, rhinitis, and asthma. IgG antibodies that grow in response to inhaling protein-filled fungi in farms, Actinomyces, and pigeon droppings are among the non-IgE mediated antibodies. Activated lymphocytes, as seen in contact dermatitis, produce allergies when they are cell-mediated. Asthma and hay fever are the most prevalent allergens.

Allergies in Children

There is a 70% chance that the child will develop an allergy if one or both parents have allergies. Children whose parents do not suffer from allergies have a considerably reduced chance (10%). Eczema (also known as dermatitis or atopic eczema) is a frequent allergy in children. It is identified by a rash on the baby's legs, arms, and face. It begins when the child is under the age of two. The rash gets more localized as the kid grows, usually around the elbows or behind the knees. Eczema affects about 20% of youngsters in the United States. Approximately 6% of the world's youngsters suffer from food allergies.

Effects of Allergies

Some of the common effects of allergies are:

  • Watery eyes
  • Sneezing
  • Itchiness in the nose, gums, soles, palms
  • Constriction in the throat
  • Rash
  • Shock and hypotension
  • Asthma
  • Difficulty in swallowing
  • Vomiting
  • Diarrhoea

Home Remedies for Allergies

Tip 1:

Honey Although there’s no scientific evidence to prove it, a popular theory suggests eating locally produced honey. According to the theory, you will lower your allergic reaction over time to the pollen that the bees collect in your area to make their honey.


Tip 2:

Air filters In your indoor environments, consider employing an air filter. HEPA filters (high-efficiency particulate air) are one type of air filter to consider. HEPA filters minimize allergens in your house by trapping airborne irritants including pollen, dust, and pet dander.


Tip 3:

Wash Up You carry small elements of the outside world into your home with you every time you enter. Your clothes, shoes, hair, and skin are covered in small particles from wherever you've been after being outside. To get rid of any allergens, take a shower and change your clothes. Also, leave your shoes at the door.


Tip 4:

Get Steamy Take a deep breath and inhale some steam. This easy method will help you breathe more easily and relieve a stuffy nose. Set a towel over your head and place it over a warm (but not too hot) bowl or sink full of water to trap the steam. Alternatively, you may sit in the bathroom while a hot shower is running.


Tip 5:

Know Your Triggers You may believe you understand the issue. But, are you certain? To pinpoint your triggers, make an appointment with an allergist for an allergy skin test. You can then devise a strategy to avoid them.
