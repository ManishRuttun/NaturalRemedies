When counting sheep doesn't work

Have you ever had one of those nights where you lay in bed wide awake as the minutes tick by and nothing you do, not even counting sheep, brings you sleep? It's not unusual for any of us to experience a restless night now and then. Insomnia, on the other hand, leaves us feeling groggy when we wake up and fatigued, irritable, and exhausted the next day. What is it that keeps you up at night, and more importantly, what can you do about it?

My brain won't shut off.

Too much on our minds can contribute to sleepless nights in our fast-paced and demanding environment. People lose sleep each night for a variety of reasons, including stress, worry, anxiety, and physical issues. Any of these factors, when combined with age, gender, or a history of depression, can lead to persistent insomnia.

My hormones are changing.

Women are more likely than males to suffer from insomnia, owing to hormonal changes that occur during menstruation, pregnancy, and menopause. All of these things can cause sleep disturbances.

I sleep in and take naps.

Napping excessively during the day or evening, spending excessive amounts of time in bed, or staying up late followed by sleeping in can all throw off your internal clock and disrupt your sleep. This can result in a sporadic and unrefreshing night's sleep.

I share the bed with a noisy or restless partner.

Co-mingling can impair your sleep, whether you're sharing a bed with a partner who snores or a noisy pet.

Insomnia that only lasts a few days may be treated by some lifestyle changes like:

  • creating a sleep routine that you stick to, even on the weekends
  • having your pet sleep in a crate or another room
  • talking with your partner about your night's sleep

It's probably time to see your doctor if it's happening most nights for at least a month. Your medical and sleep histories will be examined by him. It's possible that you'll be requested to keep a sleep journal. Long-term insomnia may necessitate treatment that goes beyond dietary and environmental changes. This entails a highly successful and brief therapy known as cognitive-behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I), which can assist you in developing the skills necessary for improved sleep.
