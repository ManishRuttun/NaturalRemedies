Your heartburn could actually be GERD

Heartburn can make it difficult to enjoy some of your favorite foods and beverages, and it can have a negative influence on your overall quality of life. Heartburn is one thing, but chronic, long-term heartburn can be an indication of a more serious condition known as gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD).

Don't be concerned. You don't have to put up with daily symptoms if you follow the appropriate treatment strategy. Continue reading to see if you have GERD and what the best treatment choices are for you.

What is GERD?

GERD is a condition in which food and stomach acid return up into your esophagus, causing reflux. The tube that links your mouth to your stomach is your esophagus. Heartburn and acid indigestion are two symptoms of GERD.

Food goes down your esophagus and into your stomach normally, passing past your lower esophageal sphincter (LES). The lower esophageal sphincter (LES) is a flap valve that joins your esophagus and stomach. It opens and shuts to allow food into your stomach. This technique may not always work well if you have GERD.

The good news is that GERD is usually not a life-threatening condition that may be managed with diet, lifestyle modifications, and medication.

Common causes of GERD

Some common causes of GERD include:

  • Cigarette smoking
  • Diet: Certain foods and beverages can weaken the LES including: chocolate, peppermint, fried or fatty foods, coffee, alcohol.
  • Hiatal hernia: part of your stomach moves into the chest through a small opening in the diaphragm. The contents of your stomach can remain above this opening.
  • Obesity
  • Pregnancy: Hormone changes and a growing uterus can crowd your intestines and stomach.

Symptoms of GERD

GERD symptoms can range from mild to severe:

  • ongoing heartburn or acid indigestion
  • bitter taste in your mouth
  • chronic coughing
  • difficulty swallowing

Test yourself for GERD to prevent potential complications or long-term health problems.

Test yourself for GERD

Do you have any of these symptoms two or more times a week?

  • heartburn or acid indigestion
  • a feeling that food is returning to your mouth
  • an acidic or bitter taste in your mouth
  • chronic coughing
  • difficulty swallowing (dysphagia)

If you answered yes to any of these questions, you might have GERD.

Treatment for GERD

  • avoiding foods and beverages that can weaken the LES such as chocolate, peppermint, fried or fatty foods, coffee and alcohol
  • avoiding foods and beverages that can irritate, such as citrus fruits and juices, tomato products and pepper
  • eating smaller meals
  • eating your last food at least two to three hours before bedtime
  • losing weight if you are overweight
  • elevating the head of your bed six inches
